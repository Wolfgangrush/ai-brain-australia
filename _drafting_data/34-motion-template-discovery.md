## Currency Warning
The Federal Court (Rule 20.11) and NSW Courts (UCPR 21.2) have moved away from "general discovery." Orders for discovery will only be made if "necessary" and must be focused on specific "categories" of documents.

## Template Type
Motion / Interlocutory Application

## Structural Skeleton
1. **Court Header:** Case details.
2. **Orders Sought:**
   - The [Party] give discovery to the [Party] of the categories of documents set out in the Schedule.
   - Discovery be provided by [Date] by filing and serving a List of Documents.
   - The List of Documents be verified by Affidavit.
   - Costs in the cause.
3. **Schedule of Categories:**
   - Category 1: [Specific description, e.g., "All contracts between X and Y"].
   - Category 2: [Specific description, e.g., "Emails regarding the 2023 price increase"].
4. **Grounds:**
   - The documents are directly relevant to the issues in the pleadings.
   - Discovery is necessary for the just, quick, and cheap resolution of the dispute.
5. **Execution:** Signature of Solicitor.

## Drafting Conventions
- **Categories vs. General:** Never ask for "all documents relating to the matter." You must define categories by reference to specific issues or time periods.
- **Verification:** The "List of Documents" (Federal Form 38 / NSW Form 43) must be verified by an affidavit from the party (not the lawyer) confirming a "reasonable search" has been conducted.
- **Technology:** For large data sets, the application should specify the use of "Technology Assisted Review" (TAR) or specific keyword searches (see CPN 1).

## Fair-Use Excerpt (if applicable)
> "The [Party] must, within 28 days, give discovery of documents in its possession, power or control in the following categories: [List]."

## Practical Use for Solo Advocate / Small Firm
The "information-gathering" phase of litigation. Solo practitioners use this to find the "smoking gun" document that the other side has not voluntarily disclosed.
