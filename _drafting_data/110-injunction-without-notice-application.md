## Currency Warning
Without-notice (ex parte) injunctions are the principal urgent remedy. Court constituted to hear urgent applications outside business hours (Duty Judge / Out-of-Hours Judge in Federal Court + each State Supreme Court). Strict full-and-frank disclosure obligation; failure leads to discharge regardless of merits (*Brink's Mat Ltd v Elcombe* [1988] principles applied — *Garrard v Email Furniture Pty Ltd* (1993) 32 NSWLR 662).

## Template Type
Application + Supporting Affidavit + Draft Orders

## Structural Skeleton

**Originating Application + Interlocutory Application (combined)**

1. **Heading** — Federal Court / State Supreme Court · case number · parties.
2. **Title** — "Interlocutory Application — Without Notice Injunction".
3. **Reliefs sought**:
   - Interlocutory injunction restraining the respondent (whether by himself, his servants, agents, or otherwise howsoever) from `<specified conduct>` until further order.
   - Liberty to apply on 48 hours' notice.
   - Inter partes return date within 7-14 days.
   - Costs of the application reserved.
4. **Undertaking as to damages** offered by applicant (see file 113).
5. **Penal notice** prominent.

**Supporting Affidavit**

1. **Deponent** — applicant / authorised representative with personal knowledge.
2. **Urgency** — why ex parte rather than inter partes:
   - Imminent threat (timing pressure).
   - Risk of respondent thwarting the relief if notice given.
   - Specific evidence of intent / capability.
3. **Serious question to be tried** — *ABC v O'Neill* threshold (broader than *American Cyanamid*'s "good arguable case").
4. **Balance of convenience** — favours grant; harm to applicant from refusal vs harm to respondent from grant.
5. **Inadequacy of damages** — equitable remedy required because damages cannot fully compensate.
6. **Full and frank disclosure** — disclosure of any matters that may weigh against grant (respondent's possible defences · alternative remedies · delay).
7. **Annexures** — contracts · correspondence · evidence of imminent harm · history.
8. **Affirmation / oath** — Statutory Declarations Act 1959 (Cth) or State equivalent.

**Draft Orders**

1. Identify the conduct restrained with precision.
2. Carve-outs (ordinary living / business expenses if Mareva).
3. Penal notice attached.
4. Return date for inter partes hearing.
5. Liberty for respondent to apply on 48 hours' notice.

## Drafting Conventions
- **Without-notice is the exception** — Federal Court strongly prefers inter partes; ex parte requires demonstrated risk.
- **Full and frank disclosure duty** — paramount.
- **Penal notice** mandatory for committal exposure.
- **Carve-outs** essential — ordinary expenses · legal fees · in Mareva context (see file 111).
- **Inter partes return** — within 7-14 days; applicant must convert ex parte order to inter partes order or it lapses.
- **Service** — within 7 days of order on respondent.
- **Genuine attempt to give notice** — even where without-notice sought, applicant should document attempts to notify (informal notice avoids later discharge applications).
- **AGLC4 citation** in submissions — *ABC v O'Neill* (2006) 227 CLR 57 + *Brink's Mat* etc.

## Fair-Use Excerpt
"The Court may, by interlocutory order, grant an injunction at any stage of the proceedings on such terms and conditions as the Court thinks fit." (Federal Court of Australia Act 1976, s 23, paraphrased.)

## Practical Use for Solo Advocate / Small Firm
The without-notice injunction is the principal urgent remedy where the respondent would otherwise frustrate the relief — typical scenarios are imminent disposal of assets, imminent publication of confidential information, imminent breach of restraint-of-trade clauses. Solo practitioners must move FAST: 48-72 hour drill — gather evidence + draft affidavit + brief counsel + secure duty-judge hearing. The full-and-frank-disclosure obligation is the principal trap.
