## Currency Warning
Federal Court "Central Practice Note (CPN 1)" emphasizes that defences should be concise and avoid "evasive denials." Practitioners should check for any list-specific practice notes (e.g., Commercial and Corporations List).

## Template Type
Pleading

## Structural Skeleton
1. **Court Header:** Consistent with the Statement of Claim.
2. **Response to Pleading Paragraphs:**
   - **Admit:** Explicitly admitting facts that are not in dispute.
   - **Deny:** Contradicting an allegation (must provide the defendant's version of facts if denying).
   - **Do Not Admit:** Where the defendant does not know if the fact is true (has the effect of a denial but is used for facts outside the defendant's knowledge).
3. **Positive Defences:** New material facts that defeat the claim (e.g., Limitation period expired, Contributory negligence, Illegality).
4. **Set-off:** Claiming a reduction in the plaintiff's claim based on a cross-debt.
5. **Particulars of Defence:** Providing necessary detail for any positive defences raised.
6. **Signing Block:** Signature of Solicitor/Counsel.
7. **Affidavit Verifying:** (Mandatory in NSW Supreme and District Courts for defences).

## Drafting Conventions
- **No Evasive Denials:** A defendant must not "deny generally" but must answer the point of substance.
- **Specific Pleading of Defences:** Statutory defences (e.g., under the *Civil Liability Act 2002*) must be specifically pleaded.
- **Inconsistency:** A defendant may plead inconsistent defences in the alternative (e.g., "The Defendant denies there was a contract, but if there was, it was not breached").

## Fair-Use Excerpt (if applicable)
> "As to paragraph 5 of the Statement of Claim, the Defendant: (a) admits the existence of the written agreement; (b) denies that the agreement contained the term alleged in sub-paragraph 5(ii); and (c) says further that the agreement was terminated by notice on [Date]."

## Practical Use for Solo Advocate / Small Firm
Used to respond to a lawsuit. A well-drafted defence narrows the issues for trial and can be the basis for a successful summary judgment or settlement negotiation.
