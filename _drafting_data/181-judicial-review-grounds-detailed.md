## Currency Warning
Australian JR grounds map to ADJR Act s 5 + common-law writs (mandamus · prohibition · certiorari). Constitutional writs available against Commonwealth officers under s 75(v) Constitution. Grounds drafting precision is critical — generic grounds struck out (*Re McBain* (2002) 209 CLR 372).

## Template Type
Pleading / Submission

## Structural Skeleton

**Grounds Template Form:**

```
GROUND <N>: [Specific ADJR Act s 5 limb / common-law equivalent]

The [decision-maker] erred in [making the decision / taking the action] in that:

(a) [Particular 1 — factual basis];
(b) [Particular 2];
(c) [Particular 3]

Particulars:
[Detailed pleading of the factual + legal basis for the ground]

Authority: [Citation of HCA / FCAFC case applying the ground]
```

**Common grounds with key authorities:**

1. **Breach of natural justice / procedural fairness**:
   - *Kioa v West* (1985) 159 CLR 550 — duty to hear before adverse decision.
   - *Minister for Immigration v Eshetu* (1999) 197 CLR 611 — exceptions.
   - Particulars: hearing rule (no opportunity to be heard / no adequate notice of adverse material); rule against bias (actual / apprehended).

2. **Procedures required by law were not observed**:
   - *Project Blue Sky Inc v ABA* (1998) 194 CLR 355 — distinguish mandatory vs directory procedures.
   - Particulars: statutory consultation not undertaken · prescribed form not used · time-limits not observed · gazettal not made.

3. **Lack of jurisdiction**:
   - *Plaintiff S157/2002 v Commonwealth* (2003) 211 CLR 476.
   - Particulars: decision-maker not authorised by statute · subject-matter outside power · delegation issue.

4. **Error of law on face of record**:
   - *Public Service Board (NSW) v Osmond* (1986) 159 CLR 656.
   - Particulars: misinterpretation of statutory provision · misapplication of legal test.

5. **No evidence to justify decision**:
   - *Bond v ABT* (1990) 170 CLR 321.
   - Particulars: no probative evidence of essential fact · decision based on fact not supported by any evidence.

6. **Improper exercise of power — failing to consider relevant matters**:
   - *Minister for Aboriginal Affairs v Peko-Wallsend* (1986) 162 CLR 24.
   - Particulars: specified matter required by statute / nature of decision · not considered.

7. **Improper exercise of power — considering irrelevant matters**:
   - *Peko-Wallsend* + state equivalents.
   - Particulars: specified matter outside statute · taken into account.

8. **Improper exercise of power — Wednesbury unreasonableness / *Li* unreasonableness**:
   - *Associated Provincial Picture Houses Ltd v Wednesbury Corp* [1948] 1 KB 223.
   - *Minister for Immigration v Li* (2013) 249 CLR 332 — modernised standard.
   - Particulars: decision so unreasonable no reasonable decision-maker could have made it.

9. **Improper exercise of power — inflexible application of policy**:
   - *Re Minister for Immigration; Ex parte Lam* (2003) 214 CLR 1.
   - Particulars: rigid application of guideline without considering individual circumstances.

10. **Improper exercise of power — acting at direction of another**:
    - *R v Anderson; Ex parte Ipec-Air* (1965) 113 CLR 177.
    - Particulars: decision-maker followed direction not authorised by statute.

11. **Bad faith** (rare ground):
    - *Sweeney v Fitzhardinge* (1906) 4 CLR 716.
    - Particulars: improper purpose · personal animus · dishonest motivation.

## Drafting Conventions
- **One ground = one error** — composite grounds struck out.
- **Pleading particulars** — each ground requires specific factual basis; bare assertion insufficient.
- **AGLC4 citation** — *Kioa v West* (1985) 159 CLR 550 + *[YYYY] HCA NN* equivalent.
- **Statement of reasons** — applicant should obtain reasons under ADJR s 13 before drafting; reasons form the factual basis for grounds.
- **Wednesbury post-*Li*** — modernised; "legally unreasonable" rather than the older "absurd" standard.
- **Constitutional writs alternative** — where ADJR not available (e.g., decisions of judicial officers), s 75(v) jurisdiction.
- **Time-limit interaction** — 28-day clock under ADJR vs reasonable time under Constitution; consider both.

## Fair-Use Excerpt
"The grounds of review under the ADJR Act mirror, and supplement, the common-law grounds for the issue of prerogative writs. Specifically, the grounds in section 5 include breach of natural justice, error of law, no evidence, and improper exercise of power including unreasonableness in the Wednesbury sense." (ADJR Act 1977 + judicial review jurisprudence, synthesised.)

## Practical Use for Solo Advocate / Small Firm
Grounds drafting is the critical task in JR. Solo practitioners should select 2-4 strong grounds with documented factual basis, not 8-12 weak ones. Each ground must have a specific statutory limb (ADJR s 5(1)(a)-(j)) + concrete factual particulars + supporting authority. The strongest grounds in modern Australian JR are procedural fairness (*Kioa*) + failing to consider relevant matters (*Peko-Wallsend*) + legal unreasonableness (*Li*).
