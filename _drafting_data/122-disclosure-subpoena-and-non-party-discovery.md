## Currency Warning
Subpoenas under FCR Part 24 issued by Registrar on filing (no judicial discretion for issue; objections after issue). Non-party discovery under FCR Part 20 Division 20.3 requires leave of Court. *Wong v Sklavos* + similar lines impose limits on subpoenas as a discovery substitute.

## Template Type
Court Form + Application

## Structural Skeleton

**(A) SUBPOENA**

1. **Form 43** (FCR) — Subpoena to Produce Documents OR Subpoena to Attend and Give Evidence OR both.
2. **Heading** — court · case number · parties.
3. **Identification of person to be subpoenaed** — name · address · capacity.
4. **Documents required** (for production subpoena):
   - SPECIFIC identification of documents.
   - Date range.
   - Categories (where specifically defined).
   - NO fishing expeditions; no "any documents relating to X" without specific bounds.
5. **Attendance details** (for attendance subpoena):
   - Date · time · location of court.
   - Conduct money tendered (per FCR Rule 24.16 — reasonable expense reimbursement).
6. **Service** — personal service per FCR Rule 24.15; minimum 5 clear days before return date (FCR Rule 24.13).
7. **Compliance** — production to court (or to issuing party if so directed) by return date.

**(B) NON-PARTY DISCOVERY APPLICATION** (where subpoena insufficient)

1. **Interlocutory Application** for leave under FCR Rule 20.23.
2. **Affidavit** establishing:
   - Documents are or are likely to be in the non-party's possession.
   - Direct relevance to issues in pleadings.
   - Necessity — cannot be obtained from a party.
   - Specific categories sought (not generic).
3. **Cost-bearing principles** — applicant typically bears non-party's reasonable costs of compliance.
4. **In camera review option** — court can inspect contested documents.

**(C) OBJECTIONS BY SUBPOENA RECIPIENT**

- **Set-aside application** — typical grounds:
  - Subpoena is oppressive (volume · cost).
  - Subpoena is being used as substitute for discovery against the issuing party.
  - Documents are privileged.
  - Documents are confidential (no privilege but warrant protective order).
  - Documents irrelevant to issues in pleadings (post-*Wong v Sklavos* abuse of process).
- **Procedure** — notice within prescribed period; court hearing.
- **Costs** — issuing party often pays recipient's costs of objection.

## Drafting Conventions
- **Specificity** — *Telstra Corp Ltd v Commonwealth* (2008) 234 CLR 210 + *Spencer Motors* line; generic subpoenas struck out.
- **Conduct money** — for individual recipients; reasonable expense reimbursement.
- **Confidentiality protections** — typical confidentiality undertaking from issuing party + restriction to use in this proceeding only.
- **Service requirements strict** — personal service · clear days · conduct money.
- **Subpoena vs non-party discovery** — subpoena requires no leave; non-party discovery requires leave.
- **Documents already discoverable from a party** — court will not permit subpoena as discovery-substitute.
- **No "scattergun"** — subpoenas in fishing expedition format struck out.

## Fair-Use Excerpt
"A subpoena to produce documents shall identify with reasonable particularity the documents required to be produced. A subpoena shall not be used as a substitute for proper discovery procedures against another party to the proceedings." (Federal Court Rules 2011 Part 24, paraphrased.)

## Practical Use for Solo Advocate / Small Firm
Subpoenas are the principal tool to obtain documents from non-parties (banks · employers · accountants · regulators · service providers). Solo practitioners must draft narrowly — categories of identified documents with specific date ranges. Wide subpoenas attract objections + costs orders. Non-party discovery applications require more procedural work but are appropriate where the documents are central to the case and the subpoena route would be challenged.
