## Currency Warning
Joint expert conferences are now routine in Federal Court complex cases. Conducted under without-prejudice protection; produces joint statement narrowing issues. Court typically directs conference + joint statement BEFORE trial; failure to confer attracts costs. Joint statement is the operative document — experts who depart from it at trial face adverse credibility findings.

## Template Type
Procedural Document / Joint Statement

## Structural Skeleton

**Pre-Conference**

1. **Court direction** for joint conference (FCR Rule 23.15) — typically at first CMC or directions hearing.
2. **Agreed list of questions** — narrow set of questions experts will address (3-10).
3. **Time + location** — typically without lawyers present (or lawyers as observers only); without prejudice.
4. **Materials** — both experts' reports + materials reviewed in those reports.

**Joint Conference**

1. **Conducted by experts** — without instructing solicitors / counsel.
2. **Discussion of questions** — each addressed; areas of agreement + areas of disagreement identified.
3. **Reasons for disagreement** — articulated.
4. **Outcomes**:
   - Agreed matters (binding for trial).
   - Disagreed matters with reasons.
   - Areas requiring further investigation.

**JOINT STATEMENT**

1. **Heading** — court · case number · parties · expert names + qualifications · date.
2. **Title** — "Joint Statement of Experts Pursuant to Order of [date]".
3. **Section 1 — Conference Particulars**:
   - Date · time · location · format (in-person / video).
   - Persons present (experts only; lawyers if permitted).
   - Documents reviewed.
4. **Section 2 — Questions Addressed** (verbatim from court direction):
5. **Section 3 — Agreed Matters**:
   - For each question: agreed answer + agreed reasons.
   - Binding for trial (subject to changes in evidence).
6. **Section 4 — Disagreed Matters**:
   - For each disputed question: each expert's position + each expert's reasoning.
   - Narrow the scope of disagreement to its essential point.
7. **Section 5 — Areas Requiring Further Investigation**:
   - Tests · data · materials not yet available.
   - Whether further investigation feasible + timeline.
8. **Section 6 — Joint Recommendations** (where appropriate):
   - Methodology to resolve outstanding disagreements.
   - Whether single joint expert appointment desirable.
9. **Section 7 — Signatures** — both experts; date.

## Drafting Conventions
- **Without-prejudice** — communications in joint conference confidential; cannot be used against either party at trial.
- **Experts only at conference** — lawyers excluded unless court orders otherwise; lawyers can observe but not interject.
- **Joint statement binding** at trial — experts who depart from joint statement face cross-examination on credibility.
- **Bullet-point format preferred** — joint statements should be concise; long discursive statements disfavoured.
- **Sequence of conferences** — for very complex cases, multiple conferences may be directed at different stages.
- **Hot-tubbing follows** — at trial, experts may be cross-examined concurrently before the bench based on the joint statement.
- **Costs of failure to confer** — adverse costs orders against the recalcitrant party.

## Fair-Use Excerpt
"The Court may direct experts to confer for the purpose of identifying matters on which their evidence will be in agreement and matters on which their evidence will not be in agreement, and producing a joint statement of those matters." (FCR 2011 Rule 23.15, paraphrased.)

## Practical Use for Solo Advocate / Small Firm
Joint conferences are mandatory in Federal Court complex litigation. Solo practitioners should engage the expert pre-conference to ensure they understand the without-prejudice nature + their overriding duty to the Court (the joint statement is NOT a negotiated settlement). The joint statement substantially narrows trial issues + drives the trial strategy. Practitioners should review the joint statement immediately on completion to identify implications for evidence + closing submissions.
