## Currency Warning
Ensure the deed is executed as a **Deed** (signed, sealed, and delivered) to avoid arguments about lack of consideration. Note the rule in *Grant v John Grant & Sons*—the release only covers matters within the actual contemplation of the parties unless expressly stated otherwise.

## Template Type
Deed / Settlement Document

## Structural Skeleton
1. **Parties:** Releasor and Releasee.
2. **Recitals:** Background of the dispute and the intention to settle.
3. **Settlement Sum:** The amount to be paid (if any).
4. **Release and Discharge:**
   - Full and final release from all "Claims."
   - Definition of "Claims" (including past, present, and future known/unknown).
5. **Bar to Proceedings:** Agreement that the deed can be pleaded as a full defence to any future action.
6. **Mutual Release:** (If applicable) where both parties release each other.
7. **No Admission of Liability:** Explicit statement that settlement is not an admission of fault.
8. **Confidentiality and Non-Disparagement:** Standard protective clauses.
9. **Tax and GST:** Responsibility for any tax consequences.
10. **Execution Block:** Formally executed as a deed with witnesses.

## Drafting Conventions
- **"Signed, Sealed and Delivered":** While the word "sealed" is no longer strictly required for individuals in most states, using the formal deed block ensures enforceability without consideration.
- **Specific vs. General:** Be as specific as possible about the "Subject Matter" to avoid the *Grant* rule narrowing the scope of the release.
- **Limitation Period:** Deeds have a longer limitation period (12 years in NSW) than simple contracts (6 years).

## Fair-Use Excerpt (if applicable)
> "The Releasor releases and discharges the Releasee from all Claims which the Releasor has, or but for this Deed would have had, against the Releasee arising out of or in connection with the Dispute." (Standard Law Society style release).

## Practical Use for Solo Advocate / Small Firm
The final document in almost every litigated or threatened dispute. Solo practitioners use this to "buy peace" for their clients and ensure that once a check is written, the matter is truly over.
