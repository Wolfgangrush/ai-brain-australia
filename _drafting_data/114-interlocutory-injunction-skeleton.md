## Currency Warning
Interlocutory injunction skeleton submissions cover the standard threshold test under *ABC v O'Neill*: (a) serious question to be tried; (b) balance of convenience favours grant; (c) damages inadequate. Modern Australian courts (post-*ABC v O'Neill*) take a flexible approach — the strength of the prima facie case is weighed against the balance of convenience as integrated factors, not separate gates.

## Template Type
Submission / Skeleton Argument

## Structural Skeleton

1. **Cover sheet** — court · case number · parties · counsel · solicitor · date · page count.
2. **Heading / Title** — "Skeletal Submissions of the Applicant for the Hearing of Interlocutory Injunction Application on `<date>`".
3. **Introduction** (1 paragraph) — relief sought in one sentence + context.
4. **Procedural posture** (1 paragraph) — stage of proceedings · prior orders.
5. **Background facts** (1-2 paragraphs) — minimum necessary; refer to affidavit for full evidence.
6. **The threshold test** (*ABC v O'Neill*):
   - "Whether the applicant has demonstrated a serious question to be tried + the balance of convenience favours grant + damages are an inadequate remedy."
7. **Serious question to be tried**:
   - Pleaded cause of action + elements.
   - Evidence supporting each element (refer to affidavit paragraphs).
   - Strength of case — characterise as "sufficiently strong" or "strong" or "exceptional".
8. **Balance of convenience**:
   - Harm to applicant if injunction refused (irreversible · quantifiable · catastrophic).
   - Harm to respondent if injunction granted (proportionate · reversible · compensable in damages on undertaking).
   - Status-quo preservation principle.
   - Third-party impact.
   - Delay (any) by applicant — addressed.
9. **Inadequacy of damages**:
   - Why money damages cannot fully compensate.
   - Trade-secret misuse · loss of customers · IP infringement · reputational harm · privacy invasion.
10. **Undertaking as to damages** offered (and fortified if appropriate) — see file 113.
11. **Form of order** — specific terms · carve-outs · return date · penal notice.
12. **Costs of application** — reserved.
13. **Anticipated objections + response** — pre-empt opposing arguments.
14. **Conclusion** (1-2 sentences).

## Drafting Conventions
- **Page limits** — typically 5-15 pages for interlocutory injunctions; verify court's PN.
- **Frame as integrated test** post-*ABC v O'Neill* — not three separate gates.
- **Quote operative passages** from authorities; AGLC4 citation.
- **Pinpoint citations** to affidavit paragraphs for every factual reference.
- **Carve-outs in proposed order** — Mareva carve-outs (file 111); search-order safeguards (file 112); restraint-of-trade carve-outs.
- **Return date discipline** — inter partes hearing within 7-14 days; failure to seek conversion may result in lapse.
- **Without notice variant** — see file 110 for additional requirements.

## Fair-Use Excerpt
"The principles governing the grant of an interlocutory injunction are well-established. The applicant must show that there is a serious question to be tried and that the balance of convenience favours the grant of the injunction. The strength of the prima facie case and the balance of convenience are considered together, not separately, and the weight to be given to each depends on the circumstances of the case." (*Australian Broadcasting Corporation v O'Neill* (2006) 227 CLR 57, paraphrased.)

## Practical Use for Solo Advocate / Small Firm
The interlocutory injunction skeleton is the workhorse document for any urgent civil application. Solo practitioners should develop a template + refine per case. Front-load the threshold test conclusion — bench should know in 30 seconds that all three limbs are met. Pre-empt the strongest counter-arguments. Pair with carefully-drafted draft orders + supporting affidavit + undertaking as to damages.
