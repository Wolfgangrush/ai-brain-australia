## Currency Warning
Voluntary Administration (VA) is Australia's principal rehabilitative insolvency procedure. Available where company is insolvent or likely to become insolvent. Appointment by:
(a) directors' resolution (s 436A — most common);
(b) liquidator (s 436B);
(c) secured creditor with security over substantially all property (s 436C).
VA followed by either Deed of Company Arrangement (DOCA — file 212) OR liquidation OR return to directors' control (rare).

## Template Type
Directors' Resolution + Notice of Appointment + Administrator's Documents

## Structural Skeleton

**(A) DIRECTORS' RESOLUTION (s 436A)**

1. **Heading** — Resolution of the Directors of `<Company>` (ACN `<number>`).
2. **Resolution** — That:
   - In the opinion of the directors, the Company is insolvent or is likely to become insolvent at some future time.
   - `<Administrator Name>` of `<Firm>` (registered liquidator under Corporations Act s 1282) is appointed as administrator of the Company under s 436A of the Corporations Act 2001.
3. **Date · location · signatures** — quorum directors.
4. **Filing** — with ASIC within 5 business days (Corporations Regs).

**(B) ADMINISTRATOR'S CONSENT TO ACT**

- Signed by administrator (in registered-liquidator capacity).
- Independence declaration per ARITA Code.

**(C) ASIC FORM 505** — Notice of Appointment of Voluntary Administrator filed with ASIC.

**(D) ADMINISTRATOR'S TIMELINE**

1. **Day 1**: Appointment effective.
2. **Day 5**: First creditors' meeting notice given.
3. **Day 8**: First creditors' meeting held — administrator confirmed; committee of creditors elected (if applicable); resolution to extend convening period (if needed).
4. **Day 20-25**: Administrator's Section 439A Report prepared — recommends DOCA / liquidation / return to directors.
5. **Day 25-30**: Second creditors' meeting — creditors vote.
6. **Outcomes**:
   - **DOCA** — administrator becomes deed administrator; company restructured (file 212).
   - **Liquidation** — administrator (or another) becomes liquidator.
   - **Return to directors** — rare; only if creditors so resolve.

**(E) MORATORIUM EFFECT (during VA)**

- Stay of creditor enforcement actions (s 440A-440J):
  - No proceedings without leave (s 440D).
  - No enforcement of charge or guarantee without leave (s 440B).
  - Repossessions stayed.
  - Lessor's rights restricted.
- **Exceptions**:
  - Court proceedings already in progress (limited).
  - Secured creditor with security over substantially all assets — protected (s 441A; "Decision Period").
  - Personal liabilities of directors not stayed.
- Administrator takes control of company; directors' powers suspended (s 437C).

## Drafting Conventions
- **5-business-day ASIC filing** — strict; non-compliance is offence.
- **Directors' personal liability** — directors must consider potential personal exposure for insolvent trading (s 588G); VA appointment may be defence + cease accrual.
- **Safe-harbour defence** (s 588GA) — directors may defend insolvent-trading liability where action taken with reasonable expectation of better outcome; documented advice + restructuring plan.
- **ARITA Code compliance** — administrator must be independent · maintain records · disclose connections.
- **Secured creditor cooperation** — early engagement with floating-charge holders essential to avoid VA being aborted by s 441A enforcement.
- **Concurrent winding-up** — VA pauses but does not preclude later winding-up if DOCA fails.
- **DOCA proposal** — administrator's Section 439A Report identifies the proposed DOCA; creditors vote on it at second meeting.
- **Pooling orders** — Corporations Act s 571 allows administration of multiple group companies; common in corporate group insolvencies.

## Fair-Use Excerpt
"A company may by resolution of its directors appoint an administrator in writing if the directors have resolved to the effect that, in the opinion of the directors voting for the resolution, the company is insolvent, or is likely to become insolvent at some future time, and an administrator of the company should be appointed." (Corporations Act 2001, s 436A, paraphrased.)

## Practical Use for Solo Advocate / Small Firm
VA is the principal alternative to winding-up for distressed but viable businesses. Solo practitioners advising distressed corporate clients should consider VA where (a) underlying business has value; (b) restructuring plan plausible; (c) cooperation from secured creditors feasible. The floating-charge-holder protection (s 441A "Decision Period") is the main practical hurdle; pre-appointment engagement with secured creditors essential. Directors' safe-harbour considerations (s 588GA) often dictate timing — too-late VA may not protect directors from prior insolvent-trading exposure.
