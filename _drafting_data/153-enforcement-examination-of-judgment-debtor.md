## Currency Warning
Examination of Judgment Debtor (EJD) is the post-judgment information-gathering tool: debtor (or, for corporate debtor, an officer with knowledge) is examined under oath about means and assets. Failure to attend or answer is contempt; committal available.

## Template Type
Application + Order + Examination Questionnaire

## Structural Skeleton

**Application for EJD Order**

1. **Heading** — court · case number · parties.
2. **Title** — "Interlocutory Application — Examination of Judgment Debtor".
3. **Reliefs sought**:
   - Order requiring judgment debtor (or, for body corporate, director / officer with knowledge) to attend at Registrar's office on `<date>` for examination on means + assets.
   - Debtor to produce specified documents (bank statements · accounts · tax returns · property documents · share certificates · vehicle ownership records · contracts in force · receivables list).
   - Costs to be added to the judgment debt.
4. **Annexures** — judgment · evidence of unsatisfied debt.

**Order for EJD** — issued by Court, served personally on the debtor.

**Examination Questionnaire** (used at hearing or filed in advance):

1. Personal / corporate identification (Tax File Number · ACN / ABN · directors).
2. Employment / business — employer · position · salary · revenue · pre-tax income for last 3 years (with ATO assessments).
3. Bank accounts — all Australian + overseas; account numbers; balances.
4. Real estate — owned · jointly owned · co-owned · leased (with Land Titles references).
5. Vehicles — registered · disposed of in last 24 months.
6. Shares + investments — listed + private companies; directorships (ASIC searches).
7. Receivables — debts owed to debtor; recoveries pending.
8. Superannuation — accumulated balance · projected at retirement (note: superannuation not directly garnishable but may be relevant for bankruptcy planning).
9. Other assets — jewellery · art · cryptocurrency · trusts in which beneficial interest claimed.
10. Liabilities — secured + unsecured debts.
11. Dispositions in last 24 months — gifts · transfers to family · sales below market value.
12. Tax filings — ATO assessments; assessable income; PAYG.
13. Expected receipts in next 12 months.
14. Family situation — spouse · dependents (for exemption analysis).

## Drafting Conventions
- **Personal service** of EJD order mandatory; non-attendance triggers contempt.
- **Corporate debtor** — director / officer with personal knowledge of company's affairs (typically finance director).
- **Pre-hearing document production** — debtor served with document list 7 days before; failure to produce = contempt.
- **Examination by judgment creditor** or solicitor — under oath; recorded by court reporter.
- **Disclosure obligations broad** — Australian courts construe means/assets broadly.
- **Privilege** — debtor may claim privilege against self-incrimination for criminal matters but NOT for civil judgments.
- **Beneficial vs legal ownership** — explore trusts · nominee arrangements · BVI/Cayman/offshore structures.
- **Worldwide asset disclosure** — Australian-resident debtors must disclose worldwide assets.
- **Follow-up applications** — after EJD, creditor uses information for Writ · garnishee · charging orders targeted at identified assets.

## Fair-Use Excerpt
"Where a judgment has been obtained for the payment of money, the Court may, on the application of the judgment creditor, order the judgment debtor or, in the case of a body corporate, any officer of that body, to attend before the Court or the Registrar to be orally examined on the questions of debts owing to the judgment debtor and other property or means of satisfying the judgment." (FCR 2011 Part 41 Division 41.5, paraphrased.)

## Practical Use for Solo Advocate / Small Firm
The most cost-effective post-judgment tool — produces information for ALL other enforcement remedies. Solo practitioners should run EJD BEFORE other enforcement steps to get the asset picture. Corporate debtors who fail to produce a knowledgeable officer face committal applications against the officers personally. EJD records are admissible in subsequent enforcement (e.g., to prove debtor lied about a particular account).
