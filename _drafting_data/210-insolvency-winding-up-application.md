## Currency Warning
Winding-up jurisdiction under Corporations Act s 461 (just and equitable) + s 459P (inability to pay debts). Federal Court + state Supreme Court concurrent jurisdiction. Threshold for statutory demand-based winding-up: A$2,000+ debt (Corporations Regulations; verify current); statutory demand non-compliance presumed inability to pay (s 459C).

## Template Type
Originating Application + Supporting Affidavits

## Structural Skeleton

**Pre-Petition — Statutory Demand under s 459E**

1. **Form 509H** — Creditor's Statutory Demand for Payment of Debt.
2. **Sum due** — must exceed prescribed minimum (currently A$2,000+).
3. **Service on registered office** of debtor company per s 109X.
4. **Period for compliance** — 21 days from service.
5. **Effect of non-compliance** — deemed inability to pay debts under s 459C; ground for winding-up application.

**Originating Application for Winding-Up**

1. **Heading** — Federal Court / state Supreme Court · case number · year · In the Matter of `<Company>`.
2. **Title** — "Originating Application for Winding-Up in Insolvency".
3. **Applicant** — creditor / contributory / liquidator / ASIC / responsible authority.
4. **Company particulars** — ACN · ABN · registered office · paid-up capital · directors.
5. **Grounds**:
   - Inability to pay debts — statutory demand non-compliance.
   - Just and equitable (s 461) — limited grounds.
   - Other s 461 grounds.
6. **Sum due + supporting documents** annexed.
7. **Reliefs sought**:
   - Order winding-up the company in insolvency.
   - Appointment of liquidator (named registered liquidator with consent) OR Official Liquidator.
   - Costs.
8. **Advertisement** — application advertised in Government Gazette + ASIC notices (per Corporations Rules); 14-day window before hearing.
9. **Service** on the company at registered office + on ASIC.

**Supporting Affidavit**

1. **Deponent** — applicant / authorised representative.
2. **Identification of debt** — invoices · judgment · contract · history of demands.
3. **Service of statutory demand + 21-day non-compliance**.
4. **Company's commercial state** — solvency analysis · ASIC searches · creditor list (if known) · public information.
5. **Bona fides** — applicant not abusing process (winding-up not for collateral purposes).
6. **Annexures** — statutory demand · proof of service · judgment / invoices · ASIC searches.
7. **Statutory declaration**.

## Drafting Conventions
- **Statutory demand strict compliance** — defective demand cannot ground winding-up.
- **Advertisement timing** — defects in advertisement may dismiss application.
- **Substantial dispute** — if debt genuinely disputed on substantial grounds, application dismissed (*Spencer Constructions Pty Ltd v G & M Aldridge Pty Ltd* (1997) 76 FCR 452); creditor should sue first.
- **Set-aside applications** — debtor may apply under s 459G to set aside statutory demand within 21 days; strict time-limit.
- **Collateral-purpose abuse** — winding-up not for debt-collection where bona fide solvent company; dismissed with indemnity costs.
- **Schemes / VA alternatives** — defendant company may apply for voluntary administration (file 211) or scheme of arrangement.
- **Effect of winding-up order** — immediate cessation of business · directors' powers cease (s 471A) · employee contracts terminated.
- **Cross-border insolvency** — UNCITRAL Model Law incorporated via Cross-Border Insolvency Act 2008 (Cth).
- **AGLC4** — *Spencer Constructions* (1997) 76 FCR 452.

## Fair-Use Excerpt
"A company is to be taken to be unable to pay its debts where the company has failed (as defined in this section) to comply with a statutory demand. A failure to comply with a statutory demand is taken to be a failure unless: (a) the company has applied to set aside the demand within the prescribed period; and (b) the demand has been so set aside." (Corporations Act 2001, Sections 459C + 459F, paraphrased.)

## Practical Use for Solo Advocate / Small Firm
The winding-up application is the principal corporate-creditor remedy for unpaid commercial debts. Solo practitioners use this most against shell or insolvent companies after judgment + unsatisfied execution. The substantial-dispute defence (*Spencer Constructions*) is the principal failure ground — petitioners should sue first on disputed debts. Statutory-demand drafting compliance is technical; even minor errors prove fatal.
