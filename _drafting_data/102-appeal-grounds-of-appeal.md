## Currency Warning
Grounds of appeal vary slightly by appellate forum but share common principles: concise · specific · framed as question of law / mixed fact and law / fact. Modern intermediate appellate courts disfavour generic grounds — *Wilson v State of Western Australia* [2007] WASCA 175 ground-specificity principles.

## Template Type
Pleading / Submission

## Structural Skeleton

**Form per ground:**
```
GROUND <N>: [Concise statement of error]

The trial Judge / lower court erred in [law / fact / mixed fact and law] in:
(a) [first sub-error — pinpoint citation to reasons below];
(b) [second sub-error];
(c) [third sub-error]

The proper conclusion, on correct application of [legal principle / fact-finding], is [appellant's position with consequence].
```

Common ground types:
- Misapplication of statutory test (cite the test + how court below misapplied).
- Failure to consider material evidence (cite paragraph of reasons + the missing evidence).
- Reliance on inadmissible evidence.
- Failure to give adequate reasons (*Soulemezis v Dudley (Holdings) Pty Ltd* (1987) 10 NSWLR 247).
- Plainly wrong finding of fact (*Robinson Helicopter Co Inc v McDermott* [2016] HCA 22 threshold).
- Procedural unfairness occasioning miscarriage.
- Error in costs assessment.
- Error in quantum (over/undercompensation).
- Error in exercise of discretion (*House v The King* (1936) 55 CLR 499 principles).

**Appellant's Submissions Structure (filed alongside grounds):**

1. **Introduction** (1-2 paragraphs) — case context · party position.
2. **Issues for determination** — bulleted (3-7 issues).
3. **Statement of facts** — agreed where possible.
4. **Decision below** — operative findings + reasoning.
5. **Grounds of appeal restated**.
6. **For each ground**:
   - Legal principle invoked.
   - Authority — HCA · FCAFC · State CoA · comparative.
   - Application to findings below.
   - Why court below erred.
7. **Disposition sought** — set aside · vary · remit · re-trial.
8. **Costs**.

## Drafting Conventions
- **One ground = one error** — composite grounds disfavoured.
- **Frame as questions** — "Whether the trial Judge erred in [X]" clarifies for bench.
- **Findings of fact** — only where *Robinson Helicopter* threshold met (plainly wrong inconsistent with incontrovertible evidence).
- **Mixed fact and law** — intermediate appellate courts more willing to intervene.
- **Discretion** — *House v The King* — must show (a) acted on wrong principle OR (b) took into account irrelevant considerations OR (c) failed to take into account relevant considerations OR (d) made plainly unjust order.
- **Pinpoint citations** to reasons below ([trial reasons] [45]-[48]) — every factual reference.
- **AGLC4 case-citation** mandatory throughout.
- **Page limits** — Full Court typically 30 pages for Appellant's Submissions; NSWCA 35 pages; verify current Practice Direction.
- **Reply submissions** — limited to new matters; 10-15 pages typical.

## Fair-Use Excerpt
"The grounds of appeal shall be stated specifically. A general ground of complaint is not sufficient." (FCR 2011 Rule 36.04, paraphrased.)

"It is for an appellant who seeks to overturn a finding of fact in the court below to demonstrate that the finding is, in plain terms, glaringly improbable, inconsistent with the incontrovertible facts, contrary to compelling inferences, or otherwise demonstrably wrong." (*Robinson Helicopter Co Inc v McDermott* [2016] HCA 22, paraphrased.)

## Practical Use for Solo Advocate / Small Firm
Grounds drafting is the single most important task in appellate practice. The Robinson Helicopter threshold + House v The King exercise-of-discretion test are the two principal litigation flashpoints. Solo practitioners drafting grounds should focus on 2-4 strong grounds rather than 8-12 weak ones. Each ground should survive the question: "If I won only this ground, would I be entitled to my full sought relief?" If not, the ground may be peripheral and dilute the appeal.
