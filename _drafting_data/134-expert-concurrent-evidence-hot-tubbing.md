## Currency Warning
Concurrent evidence (hot-tubbing) is now the default expert-evidence presentation mode in Federal Court complex commercial / IP / construction litigation. Experts give evidence concurrently under judicial questioning + cross-examination. Increases efficiency + accuracy + bench engagement. Some courts (especially Victorian Supreme Court) have institutional preference; verify per matter.

## Template Type
Procedural Document / Concurrent Evidence Protocol

## Structural Skeleton

**(A) CONCURRENT EVIDENCE DIRECTION** (typical court order)

```
1. The parties' experts on [identified issues] shall give evidence
   concurrently pursuant to Rule 23.15 of the Federal Court Rules 2011.

2. The experts shall produce a joint statement (file 132) by [date].

3. At trial:
   (a) Both experts will be sworn together.
   (b) The judge will identify the issues remaining in dispute.
   (c) Each expert will be invited to address each issue in turn.
   (d) The experts may question each other under judicial supervision.
   (e) Cross-examination by counsel follows, addressing the experts
       in sequence on each issue.
   (f) Re-examination by counsel.
```

**(B) PRE-TRIAL PREPARATION FOR HOT-TUBBING**

1. **Joint statement** finalised (file 132).
2. **Identified issues** for concurrent treatment — narrowed list.
3. **Reading materials** for the bench — both experts' reports + joint statement + key technical materials.
4. **Expert briefing** — preparing the expert for the concurrent format (different from solo cross-examination):
   - Listen actively to opposing expert.
   - Distinguish areas of genuine agreement from areas where joint statement may have over-narrowed.
   - Concede technically valid points by opposing expert.
   - Be prepared for direct questions from bench.
5. **Counsel preparation** — sequence of cross-examination different from traditional model:
   - Less aggressive (collegial professional discussion required).
   - More focused on technical analysis than credibility.
   - More integrated with bench questioning.

**(C) TRIAL PROTOCOL**

1. **Swearing-in** — both experts sworn concurrently at start of session.
2. **Judge's introduction** — issues identified; sequence outlined.
3. **Issue-by-issue treatment**:
   - Expert 1 explains position (~5-10 mins).
   - Expert 2 explains position (~5-10 mins).
   - Experts question each other (judicially supervised; ~5-15 mins).
   - Bench questions both (~5-10 mins).
   - Cross-examination by counsel (Applicant's then Respondent's; ~10-20 mins per expert).
   - Re-examination.
4. **Move to next issue**.
5. **Concluding observations** — experts may offer any final comments.

**(D) ADVANTAGES + DRAWBACKS**

- **Advantages**:
  - Bench engagement higher.
  - Genuine technical engagement between experts.
  - Reduced "hired gun" appearance.
  - Time-efficient.
- **Drawbacks**:
  - Less control by counsel.
  - Risks of one expert dominating.
  - Requires experts comfortable with collegial format.

## Drafting Conventions
- **Joint statement is foundation** — without it, concurrent evidence cannot work.
- **Counsel preparation different** — collegial cross-examination preferred; aggressive style backfires.
- **Expert preparation different** — practising the format pre-trial (mock concurrent session).
- **Bench questioning prominent** — judge actively drives the session; counsel works around bench's lead.
- **Transcript discipline** — court reporter must catch overlapping speech; chair-reading protocol helpful.
- **Hybrid formats** — concurrent evidence + traditional cross-examination on credibility-specific issues.

## Fair-Use Excerpt
"At a hearing, the Court may direct that expert witnesses give evidence concurrently, with the experts giving their opinions in sequence on each identified issue and answering questions from the Court, from each other, and from the parties' representatives." (FCR 2011 + GPN-EXPT, paraphrased.)

## Practical Use for Solo Advocate / Small Firm
Hot-tubbing is now standard in Federal Court technical-issue cases. Solo practitioners should embrace the format — properly prepared, it favours the better-reasoned expert (which is usually the one with genuine expertise rather than the "hired gun"). Counsel skills required differ from traditional cross-examination — collegial, technically engaged, integrated with bench questioning. Pre-trial mock-up sessions with the expert are essential.
