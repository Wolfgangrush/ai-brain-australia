## Currency Warning
Garnishee proceedings attach debts owed by a third party (garnishee) to the judgment debtor. Most commonly against bank accounts (banks as garnishees), trade receivables, salary, or rental payments. Two-stage in Federal Court: (1) garnishee order to show cause (ex parte); (2) garnishee order absolute (inter partes). State procedures vary.

## Template Type
Two-stage application

## Structural Skeleton

**Stage 1 — Garnishee Order to Show Cause (Ex Parte)**

*Interlocutory Application*

1. **Heading** — court · case number · parties (Judgment Creditor + Judgment Debtor + Proposed Garnishee).
2. **Title** — "Interlocutory Application — Garnishee Order to Show Cause".
3. **Reliefs sought**:
   - All debts due or accruing due from the proposed garnishee `<name + address>` to the judgment debtor attached to satisfy judgment debt of A$`<amount>` + interest + costs.
   - Garnishee to attend court on `<date>` to show cause why order should not be made absolute.
   - In the meantime, garnishee restrained from paying the debtor.
   - Costs.

*Supporting Affidavit*

1. **Deponent** — judgment creditor / solicitor.
2. **Judgment + balance outstanding** — current sum (principal + interest + costs).
3. **Existence of debt** — evidence that garnishee owes debtor (bank statement · invoice · employment contract · lease).
4. **Sum or estimated sum** held by garnishee.
5. **Annexures** — judgment · cost certificate · evidence of debt.
6. **Statutory declaration**.

**Stage 2 — Garnishee Order Absolute (Inter Partes)**

- Hearing notice served on garnishee + debtor.
- Garnishee may file affidavit disputing liability or stating amount.
- Debtor may file affidavit objecting (third-party claims · exempt funds · payment already made).
- Court makes order absolute if debt established; garnishee directed to pay creditor.

## Drafting Conventions
- **Bank account targeting** — pre-identification of bank accounts essential; ASIC searches + asset trace + bank statements from prior litigation discovery.
- **Foreign bank branches** — Australian courts cautious; situs analysis required.
- **Joint accounts** — only debtor's beneficial interest attachable; complications.
- **Salary garnishees** — limited by exemption rules (debtor must retain minimum subsistence amount under Bankruptcy Act exemption schedule by analogy).
- **Superannuation accounts** — generally NOT garnishable (Superannuation Industry (Supervision) Act 1993 protection).
- **Centrelink payments** — NOT garnishable (Social Security Act 1991 + State equivalents).
- **Solicitor's trust account** — not garnishable for unrelated debts of the client.
- **Service** — show-cause order served on garnishee + debtor; minimum 7 clear days before return date.
- **Cost of garnishee process** added to judgment debt; recoverable from debtor.

## Fair-Use Excerpt
"Where a person has obtained a judgment for the payment of a sum of money, the Court may make an order attaching any debt due or accruing due to the judgment debtor from any other person within the jurisdiction." (FCR 2011 Part 41 Division 41.3, paraphrased.)

## Practical Use for Solo Advocate / Small Firm
Garnishees on bank accounts are the most efficient post-judgment recovery tool — typically catches funds within days. Solo practitioners should obtain banking discovery during the underlying proceedings (or via EJD post-judgment) to maximise the chance of hitting an account with funds. Combine with Writ + EJD for full coverage. The 7-clear-day service rule + the exemption schedules are the principal traps.
