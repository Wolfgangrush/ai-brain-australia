## Section-by-Section Digest

### Section 6 — Interpretation (Key Definitions)

**Plain-English summary:**
Defines "personal information" and "sensitive information". These are the gateway definitions that determine if the Act applies to a specific piece of data.

**Statutory text (verbatim):**
> **personal information** means information or an opinion about an identified individual, or an individual who is reasonably identifiable:
> (a) whether the information or opinion is true or not; and
> (b) whether the information or opinion is recorded in a material form or not.
> **sensitive information** means: (a) information... about an individual’s: (i) racial or ethnic origin... (iv) religious beliefs... (ix) criminal record... (b) health information... (d) biometric information...

**Citation format:** s 6 Privacy Act 1988 (Cth)

**Cross-references:**
- Other sections: s 6FA (Meaning of health information).

**Currency:**
- Last amended: 2014 (APP introduction)

**Practical use for solo advocate:**
- First step in any privacy dispute: Is the data "personal information"? If not, the Act doesn't apply.

**STATUS:** VERIFIED

### Section 13G — Serious interferences with privacy

**Plain-English summary:**
The "big stick" penalty provision. Recent 2024 amendments removed the "repeated" requirement, making a single "serious" interference enough to trigger massive civil penalties (up to $50m for corporations).

**Statutory text (verbatim):**
> (1) An entity contravenes this subsection if:
> (a) the entity does an act... that is an interference with the privacy of an individual; and
> (b) the interference is serious.
> **Civil penalty:** (a) for a body corporate—the greater of: (i) $50,000,000; (ii) 3 times the benefit obtained... (iii) 30% of adjusted turnover...

**Citation format:** s 13G Privacy Act 1988 (Cth)

**Currency:**
- Last amended: December 2024 (Shift from "serious and repeated" to just "serious").

**Practical use for solo advocate:**
- Used for OAIC complaints and identifying high-risk breaches for clients.

**STATUS:** VERIFIED

### Section 26WH — Assessment of suspected eligible data breach

**Plain-English summary:**
Requires an entity to conduct a "reasonable and expeditious" assessment (usually within 30 days) if they suspect a data breach has occurred but haven't yet confirmed it meets the "eligible data breach" threshold.

**Statutory text (verbatim):**
> (2) The entity must:
> (a) carry out a reasonable and expeditious assessment of whether there are reasonable grounds to believe that the relevant circumstances amount to an eligible data breach...; and
> (b) take all reasonable steps to ensure that the assessment is completed within 30 days...

**Citation format:** s 26WH Privacy Act 1988 (Cth)

**Cross-references:**
- Other sections: s 26WE (Meaning of eligible data breach), s 26WL (Requirement to notify).

**Currency:**
- Last amended: 2017 (NDB scheme commencement).

**Practical use for solo advocate:**
- Key for advising clients on the "30-day window" to investigate a leak before notification is mandatory.

**STATUS:** VERIFIED

### Australian Privacy Principle 1 — Open and transparent management

**Plain-English summary:**
Requires entities to have a clearly expressed and up-to-date privacy policy. 2024 reforms now require specific transparency regarding automated decision-making (AI).

**Statutory text (verbatim):**
> 1.2 An APP entity must take such steps as are reasonable in the circumstances to implement practices, procedures and systems... that:
> (a) will ensure that the entity complies with the Australian Privacy Principles...
> [Privacy policy must be free of charge and in an appropriate form].

**Citation format:** APP 1, Schedule 1 Privacy Act 1988 (Cth)

**Currency:**
- Last amended: June 2025 (Transparency regarding ADM).

**STATUS:** VERIFIED

---

### Australian Privacy Principle 6 — Use or disclosure

**Plain-English summary:**
The "primary/secondary purpose" rule. You generally can't use or disclose personal information for a purpose other than why you collected it, unless you have consent or a specific exception (like legal requirement) applies.

**Statutory text (verbatim):**
> 6.1 If an APP entity holds personal information... collected for a particular purpose (the primary purpose), the entity must not use or disclose the information for another purpose (the secondary purpose) unless:
> (a) the individual has consented...; or
> (b) subclause 6.2 or 6.3 applies...

**Citation format:** APP 6, Schedule 1 Privacy Act 1988 (Cth)

**STATUS:** VERIFIED

---

### Australian Privacy Principle 11 — Security of personal information

**Plain-English summary:**
The core security obligation. Requires reasonable steps to protect data from misuse, loss, or unauthorized access. Also requires destruction/de-identification when data is no longer needed.

**Statutory text (verbatim):**
> 11.1 If an APP entity holds personal information, the entity must take such steps as are reasonable in the circumstances to protect the information:
> (a) from misuse, interference and loss; and
> (b) from unauthorised access, modification or disclosure.
> 11.2 ...the entity must take such steps as are reasonable in the circumstances to destroy the information or to ensure that the information is de-identified.

**Citation format:** APP 11, Schedule 1 Privacy Act 1988 (Cth)

**STATUS:** VERIFIED

### Schedule 2 — Statutory Tort for Serious Invasion of Privacy

**Plain-English summary:**
A major 2025 addition: Individuals can now sue directly for serious invasions of privacy (intrusion or misuse of info) where there was a reasonable expectation of privacy and the public interest in privacy outweighs the defendant's interest.

**Statutory text (verbatim):**
> (1) An individual (the plaintiff) has a cause of action in tort against another person (the defendant) if:
> (a) the defendant invaded the plaintiff’s privacy by: (i) intruding upon the plaintiff’s seclusion; (ii) misusing information...
> (b) reasonable expectation of privacy...
> (c) invasion was intentional or reckless;
> (d) invasion was serious;
> (e) public interest... outweighs any countervailing public interest.
> (2) Actionable without proof of damage.

**Citation format:** s 7, Schedule 2 Privacy Act 1988 (Cth)

**Currency:**
- Last amended: June 2025 (Commenced 10 June 2025).

**Practical use for solo advocate:**
- Game-changer for privacy litigation. Previously, individuals had to rely on a patchwork of other laws (trespass, breach of confidence). Now there is a direct, actionable tort.

**STATUS:** VERIFIED
