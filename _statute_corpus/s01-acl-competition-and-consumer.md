## Section-by-Section Digest

### Section 18 — Misleading or deceptive conduct

**Plain-English summary:**
Prohibits any person in trade or commerce from engaging in conduct that misleads or is likely to mislead consumers or other businesses. This is the most frequently cited section in Australian commercial litigation.

**Statutory text (verbatim):**
> (1) A person must not, in trade or commerce, engage in conduct that is misleading or deceptive or is likely to mislead or deceive.
> (2) Nothing in Part 3-1 (which is about unfair practices) limits by implication subsection (1).

**Citation format:** s 18 Australian Consumer Law

**Cross-references:**
- Other sections same statute: s 4 (misleading representations as to future matters), s 236 (actions for damages).
- Other statutes: ASIC Act 2001 s 12DA (financial services equivalent).
- Leading cases: *Google Inc v Australian Competition and Consumer Commission* [2013] HCA 1; *Miller & Associates Insurance Broking Pty Ltd v BMW Australia Finance Ltd* [2010] HCA 31.

**Currency:**
- Last amended: 2010 (Original ACL enactment)
- Notes: Stable core provision.

**Practical use for solo advocate:**
- Primary cause of action for misrepresentation. No need to prove intent or negligence; the test is the effect on the target audience.

**STATUS:** VERIFIED

### Section 20 — Unconscionable conduct within the meaning of the unwritten law

**Plain-English summary:**
Prohibits conduct that is unconscionable according to equitable principles (the "unwritten law"). Acts as a bridge to common law equity.

**Statutory text (verbatim):**
> (1) A person must not, in trade or commerce, engage in conduct that is unconscionable, within the meaning of the unwritten law from time to time.
> (2) This section does not apply to conduct that is prohibited by section 21.

**Citation format:** s 20 Australian Consumer Law

**Cross-references:**
- Leading cases: *Commercial Bank of Australia Ltd v Amadio* [1983] HCA 14 (Special disability/disadvantage).

**Currency:**
- Last amended: 2010

**Practical use for solo advocate:**
- Used when the broader statutory unconscionability in s 21 might not apply, or when relying on established equitable precedents.

**STATUS:** VERIFIED

---

### Section 21 — Unconscionable conduct in connection with goods or services

**Plain-English summary:**
Prohibits conduct that is "in all the circumstances, unconscionable" in the supply or acquisition of goods/services. This is broader than the equitable doctrine in s 20.

**Statutory text (verbatim):**
> (1) A person must not, in trade or commerce, in connection with:
>     (a) the supply or possible supply of goods or services to a person; or
>     (b) the acquisition or possible acquisition of goods or services from a person;
>     engage in conduct that is, in all the circumstances, unconscionable.
> (2) [Procedural exclusions regarding legal proceedings/arbitration]
> (3) [Foreseeability and retrospective conduct]
> (4) It is the intention of the Parliament that:
>     (a) this section is not limited by the unwritten law relating to unconscionable conduct; and
>     (b) this section is capable of applying to a system of conduct or pattern of behaviour...

**Citation format:** s 21 Australian Consumer Law

**Cross-references:**
- Other sections same statute: s 22 (interpretative factors).
- Leading cases: *ASIC v Kobelt* [2019] HCA 18; *ACCC v Quantum Housing Group Pty Ltd* [2021] FCAFC 40.

**Currency:**
- Last amended: 2021 (Clarifying "system of conduct")

**Practical use for solo advocate:**
- Powerful tool against predatory business practices. Does not require a "special disadvantage" (unlike Amadio).

**STATUS:** VERIFIED

---

### Section 23 — Unfair terms of consumer contracts and small business contracts

**Plain-English summary:**
Renders void any "unfair" term in a "standard form" consumer or small business contract.

**Statutory text (verbatim):**
> (1) A term of a consumer contract or small business contract is void if:
>     (a) the term is unfair; and
>     (b) the contract is a standard form contract.
> (2) The contract continues to bind the parties if it is capable of operating without the unfair term.
> (3) [Definition of consumer contract]
> (4) A contract is a small business contract if... [fewer than 100 persons or <$10m turnover].

**Citation format:** s 23 Australian Consumer Law

**Cross-references:**
- Other sections: s 24 (meaning of unfair), s 25 (examples of unfair terms), s 27 (standard form contract).

**Currency:**
- Last amended: Nov 2023 (Major expansion of small business definition and introduction of penalties for proposing unfair terms).

**Practical use for solo advocate:**
- Defensive shield against "take it or leave it" contracts with one-sided terms (e.g., unilateral variation, excessive termination fees).

**STATUS:** VERIFIED

---

### Section 29 — False or misleading representations about goods or services

**Plain-English summary:**
Specifically prohibits a list of false or misleading representations regarding quality, price, origin, or the existence of rights/remedies.

**Statutory text (verbatim):**
> (1) A person must not, in trade or commerce, in connection with the supply...:
>     (a) make a false or misleading representation that goods are of a particular standard, quality, value...
>     ... [m] make a false or misleading representation concerning the existence, exclusion or effect of any condition, warranty, guarantee, right or remedy...

**Citation format:** s 29 Australian Consumer Law

**Cross-references:**
- Leading cases: *ACCC v TPG Internet Pty Ltd* [2013] HCA 54.

**Currency:**
- Last amended: 2021

**Practical use for solo advocate:**
- Used for specific "hard" misrepresentations. Breach can lead to criminal penalties (unlike s 18 which is civil only).

**STATUS:** VERIFIED

---

### Section 54 — Guarantee as to acceptable quality

**Plain-English summary:**
Statutory guarantee that goods supplied to a consumer are of "acceptable quality" (fit for purpose, free from defects, safe, durable).

**Statutory text (verbatim):**
> (1) If: (a) a person supplies... goods to a consumer... there is a guarantee that the goods are of acceptable quality.
> (2) Goods are of acceptable quality if they are as: (a) fit for all the purposes... (b) acceptable in appearance... (c) free from defects... (d) safe... (e) durable...

**Citation format:** s 54 Australian Consumer Law

**Cross-references:**
- Other sections: s 2 (definition of consumer), s 259 (remedies against suppliers).

**Currency:**
- Last amended: 2010

**Practical use for solo advocate:**
- The most powerful remedy for faulty products. Replaces the old "implied terms" with non-excludable statutory guarantees.

**STATUS:** VERIFIED

---

### Section 55 — Guarantee as to fitness for any disclosed purpose etc.

**Plain-English summary:**
Guarantee that goods are reasonably fit for any purpose disclosed by the consumer to the supplier.

**Statutory text (verbatim):**
> (1) If... a person... supplies... goods to a consumer... there is a guarantee that the goods are reasonably fit for any disclosed purpose...
> (2) A disclosed purpose is a particular purpose... for which the goods are being acquired...

**Citation format:** s 55 Australian Consumer Law

**Cross-references:**
- Leading cases: *Grant v Australian Knitting Mills Ltd* [1936] AC 85 (Historical foundation).

**Currency:**
- Last amended: 2010

**Practical use for solo advocate:**
- Used when goods are "fine" generally but don't do the specific job the client told the shop they needed them for.

**STATUS:** VERIFIED
