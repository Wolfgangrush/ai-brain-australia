## Principle-by-Principle Digest

### 1. Offer and Acceptance (Unilateral Contracts)

**Plain-English summary:**
An offer can be made to the whole world. Acceptance does not always need to be communicated; performing the act requested in the offer can be sufficient.

**Leading case:** *Carlill v Carbolic Smoke Ball Co* [1893] 1 QB 256.
- **Fact Summary:** Company offered £100 reward for anyone who caught flu after using their product. Mrs. Carlill did so.
- **Legal Principle:** A unilateral contract is formed when a party performs the act required by the offer. The deposit of funds (£1,000) proved a serious intention to be bound.

**Practical use for solo advocate:**
- Useful for rewards, public offers, or "performance-based" incentives where no formal document was signed.

**STATUS:** VERIFIED

---

### 2. Implied Terms and Frustration

**Plain-English summary:**
Terms can be implied into a contract to give it "business efficacy" if they are so obvious they go without saying. A contract is "frustrated" (ended) if an unforeseen event makes performance fundamentally different from what was agreed.

**Leading case:** *Codelfa Construction v State Rail Authority of NSW* (1982) 149 CLR 337.
- **Fact Summary:** Construction limited by an injunction neither party expected.
- **Legal Principle:** Frustration occurs when performance becomes "fundamentally different". Terms will not be implied if it's not clear what the parties would have agreed (BP Refinery test).

**Practical use for solo advocate:**
- Used to end a contract without penalty when external events (e.g. government shutdown) make the job impossible or radically different.

**STATUS:** VERIFIED

---

### 3. Vitiating Factors: Unilateral Mistake

**Plain-English summary:**
A contract can be set aside if one party is seriously mistaken about a fundamental term, and the other party knows about the mistake and takes steps to ensure it isn't discovered.

**Leading case:** *Taylor v Johnson* (1983) 151 CLR 422.
- **Fact Summary:** Seller thought price was $15k/acre; buyer knew she was mistaken and kept quiet to seal the deal at $15k total.
- **Legal Principle:** Equity intervenes where one party unconscionably takes advantage of another's known mistake.

**Practical use for solo advocate:**
- A defense against "typos" or clerical errors that the other side tried to "exploit".

**STATUS:** VERIFIED

---

### 4. Vitiating Factors: Unconscionable Conduct

**Plain-English summary:**
A transaction can be set aside if a person at a "special disadvantage" is exploited by a stronger party who knows of the disadvantage.

**Leading case:** *Commercial Bank of Australia v Amadio* (1983) 151 CLR 447.
- **Fact Summary:** Elderly parents with poor English signed a guarantee for their son's failing business; bank didn't explain the risk.
- **Legal Principle:** Unconscionability requires (1) Special disadvantage and (2) Unconscientious exploitation by the stronger party.

**Practical use for solo advocate:**
- Primary tool for challenging guarantees, loans, or contracts signed by vulnerable individuals (elderly, non-English speaking, etc.).

**STATUS:** VERIFIED

---

### 5. Intermediate Terms and Termination

**Plain-English summary:**
A party can terminate a contract if a term is breached that is "intermediate" (neither a condition nor a warranty) and the breach is so serious it goes to the "root of the contract".

**Leading case:** *Koompahtoo Local Aboriginal Land Council v Sanpine* (2007) 233 CLR 115.
- **Fact Summary:** Failure to keep financial records in a joint venture.
- **Legal Principle:** Breach of an intermediate term justifies termination if it deprives the innocent party of a "substantial part of the benefit".

**Practical use for solo advocate:**
- Justification for "walking away" from a contract when the other side has consistently failed in important, but technically "non-essential", administrative ways.

**STATUS:** VERIFIED

## Cross-statute Relationships
- **Australian Consumer Law (ACL):** Statutory prohibitions on unconscionable conduct (s 21) and unfair contract terms (s 23) often override or supplement these common law rules.
- **Electronic Transactions Acts:** Validation of electronic signatures and offer/acceptance in the digital age.
