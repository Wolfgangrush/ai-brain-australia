## Currency Warning
Stable principles but increasingly emphasized due to an aging population and "Elder Abuse" awareness.

## Key Facts
1. **The Presumption:** Every adult is presumed to have the capacity to make their own decisions unless proven otherwise.
2. **The Solicitor's Role:** A solicitor must be satisfied that the client has the mental capacity to give instructions.
3. **Tests for Capacity:** Varies depending on the transaction (e.g., *Banks v Goodfellow* test for Wills; "Informed Consent" for litigation).
4. **Red Flags:** Memory loss, confusion, undue influence by a third party (e.g., a family member present during conferences).
5. **External Assessment:** If in doubt, the solicitor should seek a professional medical assessment (with the client's consent).

## Verbatim Quote (if applicable)
"A practitioner should not follow the instructions of a client if they have reasonable grounds to doubt the client’s capacity to give those instructions." — Law Society Guidance.

## Practical Implication for Solo Advocate
- Solos in Wills & Estates must document their "Capacity Assessment" in every file note.
- "Elder Abuse": Solos should insist on seeing elderly clients alone to screen for undue influence.
- If a solo proceeds with a transaction where the client lacked capacity, they may be liable for professional negligence or a breach of ethical duties.
