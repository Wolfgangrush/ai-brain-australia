## Currency Warning
Uniform Commercial Arbitration Acts are stable. The National Mediator Accreditation System (NMAS) is currently being rebranded to the Australian Mediator Accreditation System (AMAS) in 2024-2025.

## Key Facts
1. **Commercial Arbitration:** Uniform state laws (based on UNCITRAL Model Law) provide for the conduct of domestic arbitration. Courts have limited power to intervene.
2. **The "Agreement to Arbitrate":** Must be in writing.
3. **Finality:** Arbitral awards are generally final and binding with very limited grounds for appeal (errors of law only if agreed).
4. **Mediation (NMAS/AMAS):** The national standard for accrediting mediators. Most court-ordered mediations require an accredited mediator.
5. **Confidentiality:** Both arbitration and mediation are private and generally confidential (unlike open court).

## Verbatim Quote (if applicable)
"The court must... refer the parties to arbitration unless it finds that the agreement is null and void, inoperative or incapable of being performed." — Section 8, Commercial Arbitration Act.

## Practical Implication for Solo Advocate
- Solos should ensure commercial contracts for clients include an "Arbitration Clause" to avoid the cost and delay of public litigation.
- Becoming an "Accredited Mediator" is a common way for senior solo advocates to diversify their income.
- Confidentiality in ADR is a major selling point for solo practitioners' high-net-worth or corporate clients.
