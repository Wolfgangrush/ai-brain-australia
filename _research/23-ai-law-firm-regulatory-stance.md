## Key Facts
1. **Duty of Competence:** AI use does not diminish the solicitor's personal responsibility for the accuracy of court filings.
2. **Hallucination Risk (*Dayal* case):** A solicitor was referred to the regulator for failing to verify "hallucinated" cases generated by AI.
3. **Confidentiality:** Inputting client data into public/unsecured AI models is a likely breach of LPUCSR Rule 9.
4. **Law Council Stance:** Prefers "mandatory guardrails" for high-risk AI applications over a general AI Act.
5. **Human-in-the-loop:** The common regulatory thread is that a human practitioner MUST review and "sign off" on all AI-assisted work product.

## Verbatim Quote (if applicable)
"The duty of a legal practitioner to the court is paramount... the use of artificial intelligence technology does not alleviate a practitioner’s obligation to ensure that the authorities they cite are accurate and relevant." — *Dayal v State of New South Wales* [2024] FedCFamC2F 1166.

## Practical Implication for Solo Advocate
- Never copy-paste AI output directly into a court document without primary source verification.
- Solos must ensure their AI tools are "Enterprise Grade" with data privacy guarantees (no training on user data).
- Disclosure: Some courts may require a statement if AI was used to prepare submissions (check specific Practice Directions).
