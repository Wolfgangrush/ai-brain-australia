## Currency Warning
Legal Profession Uniform Conduct (Solicitors) Rules 2015 apply in NSW, VIC, and WA. Other states have similar but distinct rules (e.g., ASCR).

## Key Facts
1. **Rule 9.1:** A solicitor must not disclose any information which is confidential to a client and acquired by the solicitor during the client’s engagement.
2. **Duration:** The duty continues after the engagement has ended.
3. **Exceptions (Rule 9.2):**
    - Client authorizes disclosure.
    - Solicitor is permitted or compelled by law.
    - Disclosure to avoid the probable commission of a serious criminal offence.
    - Disclosure for the purpose of preventing imminent serious physical harm to the client or another person.
    - Disclosure for obtaining ethical advice.
4. **Interaction with tech:** Use of cloud services must not compromise this duty (requires adequate security/encryption).

## Verbatim Quote (if applicable)
"A solicitor must not disclose any information which is confidential to a client and acquired by the solicitor during the client’s engagement to any person who is not: 9.1.1 a solicitor who is a partner, principal, director, or employee of the solicitor’s law practice; or 9.1.2 a barrister or an employee of, or person otherwise engaged by, the solicitor’s law practice..." — Rule 9.1

## Practical Implication for Solo Advocate
- Unlike a firm, a solo has no partners to "automatically" share with; disclosure to any third-party (e.g., external bookkeeper or IT provider) requires careful management and confidentiality agreements.
- Cyber breaches (e.g., due to failing Essential Eight) can lead to a professional misconduct charge for breach of Rule 9.
- Data localization (s 77 Health Records) is a subset of this duty when health data is involved.
