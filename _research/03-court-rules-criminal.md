## Currency Warning
Criminal procedure is heavily state-based. Federal criminal matters follow the *Crimes Act 1914* and the *Judiciary Act 1903*.

## Key Facts
1. **Summary vs. Indictable:** Summary offences are heard in the Magistrates/Local Court; indictable offences are heard in District/Supreme Court after a committal hearing.
2. **Disclosure:** The prosecution has a heavy duty to disclose all relevant evidence to the defense, including evidence that might help the accused.
3. **The Caution:** Statements made by an accused person are generally only admissible if they were made after a proper caution (s 139 Evidence Act).
4. **Bail:** Governed by state-based Bail Acts (e.g., *Bail Act 2013* NSW); focuses on "unacceptable risk" to the community or of flight.
5. **Committal Proceedings:** A preliminary hearing to determine if there is enough evidence for a trial in a higher court.

## Verbatim Quote (if applicable)
"The prosecutor must disclose to the accused person all information that the prosecutor is aware of... that is relevant to the case for the prosecution or the case for the accused person." — Section 142, Criminal Procedure Act 1986 (NSW).

## Practical Implication for Solo Advocate
- Solos in criminal law must be vigilant in chasing "Briefs of Evidence" to ensure full disclosure.
- Bail applications are often the first (and most critical) engagement for a solo practitioner in a criminal matter.
- Understanding "Sentencing Guidelines" and "Plea Deals" is essential for advising clients in high-volume summary matters.
